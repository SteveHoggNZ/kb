# Commitment Loops

Every promise you make opens a loop in your brain. Until that task is completed, the loop buzzes in the background — consuming cognitive space, creating anxiety, degrading focus. The highest-ROI use of AI agents is **closing these loops immediately** by delegating to completion, not to draft.

> **The one-sentence version:** Don't use AI to help you do the work. Use it to do the work so you don't have to.

---

## The Mental Buzzing

Throughout the workday, you make promises across every channel — email, Slack, WhatsApp, meetings:

- "I'll send the revised scope by Thursday"
- "I'll get the meeting minutes to you by tomorrow"
- "I'll sign the MOU by Wednesday"

Each promise opens a **commitment loop**. Until completed, that promise sits in your head taking up cognitive space. The accumulation of open loops creates a persistent background buzzing — the feeling that something is undone, even when you can't name what.

This is [[Monkey Management]] applied to yourself: every open commitment is a monkey on *your* back.

---

## Pseudo-Work vs Real Work

The distinction that determines whether AI actually closes your loops or just reshapes them:

### Pseudo-Work (Lands ON Your Desk)

AI outputs that masquerade as productivity but create more busywork for you. The loop stays open.

| Pseudo-Work Pattern | Why the Loop Stays Open |
|-------------------|----------------------|
| "Here's a proactive briefing for your meeting" | Just another doc to read |
| "Here's a draft outline of the scope" | You still have to finish, polish, and send it |
| "Here are three approaches you could take" | You still have to choose, execute, and deliver |
| "I've summarised the email thread" | You still have to decide what to do about it |

**The test:** After the AI finishes, is the task *done* or do you still need to sit at your desk? If you still need to act, it's pseudo-work. The AI changed your work from *writing* to *reading/editing*, but the loop is still open.

### Real Work (Gets OFF Your Desk)

AI executes the task to completion. You walk away. The loop closes.

| Real Work Pattern | Why the Loop Closes |
|------------------|-------------------|
| "Research the competitor's patents, write the report, send it to the team" | Task is done. Report is delivered. |
| "Log into the CRM, pull Q4 data, compile the spreadsheet, email it to accounting" | Data is extracted and delivered without you touching it. |
| "Write the revised scope, format it, send it to the client" | Promise fulfilled. Client has the deliverable. |
| "Monitor airline prices. Alert me only if they drop below $400" | Continuous task running in the background while you sleep. |

**The metric:** "A summary for you to read" is pseudo-work. "A report sent for you" is real work.

## The Loop-Closing Protocol

Closing commitment loops requires pairing the **Manager Mindset** with **Real Work**:

```
1. You make a promise              → Loop opens
2. Immediately pull out your phone → Don't carry the monkey
3. Dispatch to agent with full intent and instructions
4. Agent executes to completion    → Real Work, not pseudo-work
5. You walk away, trusting the system → Loop closes
```

The critical step is **3 → 4**: the instruction must be specific enough for the agent to execute to completion. "Draft an outline" keeps the loop open. "Write the document, format it as PDF, and email it to sarah@client.com" closes it.

If the AI's output isn't good enough, **don't take the task back**. View it as a prompting or skill issue. Learn to give the agent better context so the loop stays closed. Taking the task back reopens the loop and defeats the purpose.

---

## Why Pseudo-Work Persists

Most AI demos optimise to "look good" rather than actually solve problems. The industry defaults to pseudo-work because:

1. **It's safer** — a draft you review feels less risky than a document sent autonomously
2. **It's easier to demo** — "Here's a briefing!" is more photogenic than "Task completed silently in the background"
3. **It matches old habits** — we're used to AI as an assistant, not a delegate
4. **Trust isn't built yet** — users don't believe the agent can handle completion

Overcoming this requires pushing toward higher [[Agent Autonomy Levels]] — from Level 3 (recommend, wait for approval) to Level 4-5 (act then report, act independently). The [[Agentic Primitives]] (Scheduled Tasks, Dispatch, Computer Use) are the tools that make Level 4-5 practical.

## Connection to Existing Concepts

### Monkey Management for the AI Era

[[Monkey Management]]'s core insight: every time you say "let me think about it," the monkey jumps to your back. Commitment Loops extends this: every time you say "I'll handle that," a loop opens in your brain.

The original solution was to push monkeys back to subordinates. The AI-era solution is to push them to agents — immediately, from your phone, to completion.

| Monkey Management (1974) | Commitment Loops (2026) |
|--------------------------|------------------------|
| Push monkeys to subordinates | Push loops to agents |
| "Draft a proposal and we'll review Thursday" | "Write the proposal, format it, send it to the client by Thursday" |
| Manager's job: manage monkey flow | Your job: manage loop flow |
| Bottleneck: manager accumulates tasks | Bottleneck: you accumulate open promises |

### The Manager Mindset

[[The 2026 Builder Operating System]]'s Engineering Manager Mindset is exactly this skill. You don't write the code — you manage the agents that write it. You don't fulfil the promise — you manage the agent that fulfils it.

### Decompose-Route-Recompose at the Individual Level

[[Decompose-Route-Recompose]] operates here too: decompose the commitment into research (agent) and judgment (you). The agent gathers deep context *before* you need to decide. You make the final call. The loop closes when the decision is executed, not when the research arrives.

### Intent Engineering Connection

For loops to close reliably at scale, agents need [[Intent Engineering]] — enough encoded judgment to handle completion without constant supervision. Without intent, you're back to pseudo-work: the agent drafts, you review, the loop stays open.
