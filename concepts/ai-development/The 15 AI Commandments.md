# The 15 AI Commandments

An operating system for making a company ship at the pace of AI-native startups. The core move: **push repeatable human coordination into code**, so humans are freed to spend their time on judgment, taste, and speed.

The commandments are not a menu. They form an **interconnected system** — you can't adopt one rule (e.g. "kill roadmaps") without the others that make it safe (e.g. "product works in the terminal daily"), or the structure collapses. This is the ways-of-working companion to [[The 2026 Builder Operating System]] and a concrete enactment of [[The Great Inversion]]: when execution is cheap, the old coordination scaffolding becomes the bottleneck.

---

## The three systems

The 15 group into three clusters that must reinforce each other.

### System 1 — Product in Code

*Commandments 1, 3, 4, 5, 6, 12, 13.*

Removes the traditional bottlenecks of roadmaps and distant product management. PMs and designers work **directly in the code / terminal**, alongside engineers, daily — testing and iterating on the customer experience rather than specifying it from afar. Engineering time is protected from being directed by product; the two *jam together* instead. Design and product decisions live in the code, not in decks or separate UI mockups.

This is why "Documentation is code" and "Design is in the code" matter: intent has to be where the work happens, echoing the context-engineering discipline in [[The New SDLC with Vibe Coding]] (rule files and specs as the real interface to agents).

### System 2 — Writing

*Commandments 2, 7, 11.*

Replaces long, low-value, and standing meetings with **rigorous, clear documentation**. Writing isn't bureaucracy here — it's a hard requirement, because AI agents need precise written intent to execute well. Clear writing is the substrate both humans and agents run on. This directly attacks the "meetings as default" habit called out in [[Eight Habits to Break]].

### System 3 — Human Culture

*Commandments 8, 9, 10, 14, 15.*

Ensures human behaviour can survive the rapid pace: adaptability ("like water over stone"), assuming best intent, bringing complete fixes instead of complaints, building with a team rather than alone, and a learn/teach norm (imitate those going faster, teach those going slower). As technical output commoditises, this cultural layer — trust, teamwork, generosity — becomes the durable advantage, the same premium on the human element noted in [[The Great Inversion]] and grounded in [[Trust]].

## The 15 commandments

1. **Thou shalt not slow down engineering** — thou shalt go fast.
2. **No meeting longer than an hour.**
3. **Product does not make roadmaps.**
4. **Product shall be in the terminal daily.**
5. **Product does not control or direct engineering time.**
6. **Product shall sit with engineering and jam daily.**
7. **Kill low-value standing meetings** — audit your monthly commitments.
8. **Stay flexible to deliver value** — like water over stone.
9. **Assume best intent.**
10. **Thou shalt not complain** — fix it, or propose a complete fix.
11. **Documentation is code.**
12. **Design is in the code** — not just the UI.
13. **All that matters is one profoundly helpful, delightful customer experience.**
14. **Thou shalt not build alone** — build with a team.
15. **Imitate those going faster; teach those going slower.**

## Why it has to be a system

The speaker's central caution: this is an operating system, not a pick-list. Eliminating roadmaps (3) only works if product is actually in the terminal (4) and jamming with engineering daily (6). Killing meetings (2, 7) only works if writing (11) carries the intent that meetings used to. Protecting engineering speed (1, 5) only works if a strong human culture (8–15) keeps trust and coordination intact without the old ceremonies. Remove one leg and the others buckle — the failure mode is adopting the *permissions* (fewer meetings, no roadmaps) without the *disciplines* (writing, terminal presence, culture).

### [[Eight Habits to Break]]
System 2 (Writing) and the anti-meeting commandments directly counter the "meetings as default," "structured waiting," and "deck instead of demo" habits.

### [[The New SDLC with Vibe Coding]]
