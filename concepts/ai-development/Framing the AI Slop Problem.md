# Framing the AI Slop Problem

We're framing the AI slop problem incorrectly. The slop isn't new — it's the same pattern we've seen with every democratisation of communication tools, amplified.

---

## The Printing Press Parallel

Universal literacy was one of humanity's great achievements. But most of what humans write isn't the Count of Monte Cristo. The printing press and universal education created an explosion of human output, and most of that output was — by definition — average. Mediocre memos, forgettable letters, reports nobody wanted to read. Ostraca — scribblings on potsherds.

We just didn't have to confront it at scale because distribution was expensive and slow, and because most of our work was scribblings that disappeared within a few years.

AI amplifies this. When you give everyone a tool that produces written output at near-zero cost, you get a lot of average written output. This is the same dynamic [[Scarcity to Abundance - A Unifying Lens]] describes: abundance changes the game, but the human instinct is to evaluate it with scarcity-era thinking.

---

## The Cognitive Load Shift

**AI slop moves the cognitive load from the person generating the content to the person reading it.**

This is the core of why people hate it. Previously, the writer bore the cost of production — thinking, drafting, editing. Now the writer can externalise that cost to a model, but the *reader* still has to parse, evaluate, and filter. The burden hasn't disappeared; it's shifted downstream.

This connects directly to [[The Bottleneck Economy]]: the bottleneck moves. When production was expensive, the constraint was on the supply side. Now that production is near-free, the constraint shifts to the demand side — the reader's attention, trust, and ability to distinguish signal from noise. Attention is the new scarce resource, and slop wastes it.

---

## Why Slop Isn't Inevitable

The slop comes from connecting a model to a publish button and hoping for the best. That's [[Conditioning the Distribution]] at its worst — generic prompts producing regression-to-the-mean output. The hallucinated confidence, the uncanny valley of almost-helpful content.

When you build actual systems around the model, you can produce work that's dramatically better than what most humans produce unassisted:

- **Retrieval** to ground the claims
- **Validation** to catch the errors
- **Human checkpoints** for edge cases
- **[[Taste in Software|Taste]]** applied at some point in the process

The difference isn't the model; it's everything around the model. This is [[The Great Inversion]] in action: execution (generation) is cheap; clarity (knowing what's worth generating and how to check it) is expensive.

---

## The Quality Test

Three questions that separate signal from slop:

1. **Is the content information-dense?**
2. **Is it something you can come back to?**
3. **Does it respect your time?**

These are the right questions, and AI can help you answer them well — if you build the systems to make it happen. [[Teach the Delta]] applies here: the best AI-assisted content teaches you something you didn't already know. Slop just re-states the baseline.

---

## Selection Pressure

Humans have a pretty reliable history of finding great works (like Shakespeare) out of the sea of slop we ourselves have written. Good stuff rises to the top.

The builders and marketers who figure out how to produce quality at scale are going to eat the lunch of the ones who just shipped volume. Selection pressure is real. [[The Barbell Economy]] predicts this: Layer 1 output (tokenisable, commoditised) gets competed to zero. Layer 2 judgment — knowing what's worth making, what's worth reading, what respects the audience — is where value concentrates.

Humans are hungry for real stories and real dense content. The slop era is a transition, not a destination.
