# Psychological Principles in UX Design

The best UX/UI design goes beyond aesthetics — it works *with* how the brain evaluates risk, effort, and value. Six psychological principles that measurably improve experience, engagement, and conversion.

These are applied cousins of the reasoning shortcuts and biases catalogued in [[Logical Fallacies]]: the same mental heuristics, turned from *errors to avoid in arguments* into *levers to design with*. Used honestly they reduce friction; used cynically they become dark patterns — the ethical line is whether the nudge serves the user's goal or only the business's.

---

## Tip 01 — Smart Defaults `00:22`

Blank forms cause **decision fatigue** and raise abandonment. Pre-fill fields with the most common choice; users read a sensible default as a trusted recommendation, which lowers friction and speeds completion.

## Tip 02 — Goal Gradient Effect `01:45`

People accelerate toward a goal as they get closer to it. In onboarding, **never start the user at 0%** — give an artificial head start (e.g. "20% complete" just for downloading the app). The manufactured momentum sharply reduces drop-off.

![[ux-tip-02-goal-gradient.png]]

---

## Tip 03 — Reciprocity `04:55`

The instinct to return a favour once given something first. Demanding sign-up before showing value feels like a barrier. Give free value upfront — a partial report, a free trial — and users feel far more inclined to create an account in return.

![[ux-tip-03-reciprocity.png]]

---

## Tip 04 — Endowment Effect (the IKEA Effect) `06:06`

People value what they helped build or customise. Instead of a generic signup wall, let users start setting up a profile, picking colours, or doing a first lesson *before* asking them to sign up. Invested effort creates a sense of ownership, and they're less likely to abandon.

![[ux-tip-04-endowment-effect.png]]

---

## Tip 05 — Loss Aversion `08:19`

The pain of losing is roughly twice as powerful as the pleasure of gaining. Rather than pitching premium features purely as a "gain," frame the potential **loss** (e.g. "Your oldest files will be deleted in 3 days"). Loss framing motivates action more strongly than equivalent gain framing.

![[ux-tip-05-loss-aversion.png]]

---

## Tip 06 — Contrast Effect `10:28`

Prices aren't judged in isolation — they're compared to whatever was seen just before. Show a larger **anchor** first (the yearly cost, or the full price of a laptop) so a smaller add-on ($49/mo for protection) feels affordable by comparison.

![[ux-tip-06-contrast-effect.png]]

---

## The through-line

Each tip re-frames a decision so the brain's shortcut works *for* the user rather than against completion: defaults cut effort, the goal gradient manufactures momentum, reciprocity and endowment build investment before the ask, and loss aversion and contrast reshape how value and price are perceived. Sequencing the *ask* after the *investment* is the recurring move — a design analogue to reducing perceived risk before requesting commitment.
